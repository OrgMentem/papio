Abstract

When do consumers trust artificial intelligence (AI)? With the rapid adoption of AI technology in the field of marketing, it is crucial to understand how consumer adoption of the information generated by AI can be improved. This study explores a novel relationship between number presentation details associated with AI and consumers' behavioral and evaluative responses toward AI. We theorized that consumer trust would mediate the preciseness effect on consumer judgment and evaluation of the information provided by AI. The results of five studies demonstrated that the use of a precise (vs. imprecise) information format leads to higher evaluations and behavioral intentions. We also show mediational evidence indicating that the effect of number preciseness is mediated by consumer trust (Studies 2, 4, and 5). We further show that the preciseness effect is moderated by the accuracy of AI-generated information (Study 3) and the objective product quality of the recommended products (Study 4). This study provides theoretical implications to the AI acceptance literature, the information processing literature, the consumer trust literature, and the decision-making literature. Moreover, this study makes practical implications for marketers of AI businesses including those who strategically use AI-generated information.

1 INTRODUCTION

For years, advances in artificial intelligence (AI) have radically shifted how marketing strategies are implemented in terms of customer service, business relationships, and sales management (Bock et al., 2020; Kaartemo & Helkkula, 2018; Lu et al., 2020; Martínez-López & Casillas, 2013). Recommendation systems are an efficient way for companies to suggest products and services to customers based on data analysis of their personal preferences and behaviors. The use of AI-based recommendation agents in the online marketplace is increasingly prevalent (Davenport et al., 2020). For example, Netflix displays a narrower selection of TV shows and movies that people are expected to enjoy rather than presenting their entire catalog to each viewer. This contributes to the overall experience of consumers and results in considerable gains for the company. In addition, companies like Amazon.com integrate recommendations into the purchasing process, and these systems have expanded into several other domains such as retail, travel, and finance.

Many retailers and service companies are using AI to understand their customers' preferences and desires, with varying levels of success and accuracy. Despite the expanding adoption of AI and increasing use of AI-based recommendation agents, our understanding of consumers' cognitive reactions toward these emerging technologies is still limited. Prior research has demonstrated consumer resistance toward adopting AI recommendations (e.g., algorithm aversion, see Dietvorst et al., 2015). In addition to privacy and regulatory issues, this stream of research suggests that consumers do not trust AI's ability to make accurate inferences regarding their preferences (Dietvorst et al., 2015; Longoni et al., 2019). A lack of sufficient data and information can lead to inaccurate recommendations from the AI recommendation system or agent. In addition, previous research in healthcare and customer support has shown that individuals may experience a great level of discomfort and perceived risk when dealing with AI robots and recommendation systems (Huang & Rust, 2018). Adoption and acceptance of new technologies can be complex as those innovations come with many changes that often translate into higher levels of perceived risk and uncertainty for individuals. Thus, a significant research question is how to increase the understanding of when consumers trust AI agents' recommendations. Extant research has found several factors that increase trust toward AI. For example, consumers respond to AI more favorably when it is used to support the analytical (vs. intuitive) aspects of decision-making (Jarrahi, 2018), but consumers react to AI less favorably when the decisions are more intuitive and of the “common-sense” type (Guszcza et al., 2017; McAfee et al., 2012).

Meanwhile, other research has suggested the opposite pattern of consumer trust in AI recommendations: algorithm appreciation (Logg et al., 2019). Indeed, the algorithmic judgments of AI systems are often more accurate and precise than human assessments (Dawes et al., 1989). With large amounts of data, these behavioral signals can become insights for companies, and greater personalization of offers and services can be achieved, which are then perceived to be more suited for consumers, ultimately leading to more purchase intentions, engagement, and click-through conversions (Aguirre et al., 2015). Therefore, variables that can guide the usage of AI recommendations in decision-making must be determined.

Given the recent substantial developments in the area of AI and AI-generated recommendations, the objective of this paper is threefold: (a) examine conditions under which individuals are more willing to accept AI suggestions and recommendations, (b) investigate the impact of information preciseness on individuals' reactions to AI, and (c) examine how trust affects this process. Specifically, we focus on the nudging way of improving AI recommendations by examining the preciseness of the recommendation information. The existing literature suggests that people believe that AI currently has good skills for mechanical and analytical tasks as opposed to intuitive and empathetic ones (Huang & Rust, 2018). One salient characteristic of mechanical and analytical tasks could be “precise” or “logical and analytic.” Therefore, we can expect that an AI recommendation will be evaluated higher when the preciseness of the recommendation communication is precise (vs. nonprecise). For example, when forecasting the next day's probability of rain, people might prefer an AI recommendation containing precise information (e.g., 59.85% chance of rain) to one containing imprecise or rounded information (e.g., 60% chance of rain). We further suggest that user trust in AI recommendations drives the positive effect of AI recommendations providing precise information.

Accordingly, this study makes several significant theoretical and managerial contributions. First, it contributes to the marketing and usage of technologies, specifically AI literature, by examining a factor that can influence consumer reactions to AI recommendation systems. This is significant because prior research has obtained contradictory findings on preferences and trust regarding AI recommendations. Indeed, by incorporating the notion of information preciseness, we contribute to a better understanding of how cognitive processing contributes to the acceptance of AI recommendations. Second, we advance trust as a crucial construct for understanding people's behaviors toward AI. With new technologies, individuals can experience discomfort and uncertainty, and developing trust toward AI plays a pivotal role in the adoption and acceptance. We show that the preciseness of the information provided influences the level of trust and that this trust can impact preferences and decisions related to AI recommendations. This provides an answer about how cognitive trust can be developed between humans and AI. In addition, our research expands the literature in behavioral economics by showing that AI-generated recommendations represent an anchor in consumer evaluations and decision-making processes. Finally, the results of this study have substantive implications. Although AI is revolutionizing the business world and that consumers are exposed to an increasing amount of AI-generated content, more research is needed to provide guidance in terms of the best ways to communicate with individuals. This paper contributes to our understanding of how to frame information to increase acceptance of AI recommendations. The findings have straightforward implications for companies to make recommendations successfully and enhance consumer experiences with their organizations.

2 THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK AND PREDICTIONS

2.1 AI technologies in business and marketing

AI elates to “programs, algorithms, systems and machines” (Shankar, 2018, p. vi) that manifest and imitate elements of human intelligence and behavior (Huang & Rust, 2018; Syam & Sharma, 2018). AI uses various technologies, including machine learning, natural language processing, deep learning, big data analysis, and physical robots (Davenport et al., 2020; Mariani, 2019; Mariani et al., 2018). AI technologies have developed considerably over the past decades and are becoming an important part of consumers' daily lives.

In business, AI technologies allow for the efficient processing of vast amounts of data and the learning of relationships among the data, potentially leading to consumer insights and enhanced personalization. The use of AI can significantly enhance various functions of a business, including the automation of business processes; the acquisition of marketing insights from data, engagement, and interaction with customers and employees; the development of planning strategies, such as segmentation and analytics, predictions of trends and consumer behavior; and the recommendations made to customers (Davenport et al., 2020). Companies from various industries, including healthcare (Longoni et al., 2019; Zang et al., 2015), military (Amoroso et al., 2018) and hospitality (Adomavicius & Tuzhilin, 2005) adopt AI technology, and they used it in various usage contexts including voice assistant (Moriuchi, 2019) and companion robots (Davenport et al., 2020).

In the context of marketing, AI is revolutionizing how companies and organizations create content, advertise and make recommendations (de Ruyter et al., 2018). Marketers must understand how to best integrate AI in businesses and contribute to individual's higher acceptance to maintain a competitive advantage. Given the arrival of AI-generated marketing, a critical research question is to understand whether and how consumers accept AI-generated contents and information. In the past decade, researchers have identified potential issues and areas to develop to gain a deeper understanding. For instance, Bock et al. (2020) explored the internal and external values provided by integrating AI technologies in the service environments. Technologies, such as AI and robots, are critical agents in the value cocreation and technology engagement and experience (Kaartemo & Helkkula, 2018). Technological developments shape individuals' motivations, expectations, and concerns, and marketers must comprehend better how they can influence and alter consumer behaviors and experiences (Lu et al., 2020). Based on the key issues identified around the management of consumers' relationships and engagement (Lu et al., 2020; Martínez-López & Casillas, 2013), this paper generates a better understanding of how AI can be a critical factor in the transformation of behaviors. Moreover, it examines how cognitive processing influences the acceptance of those technologies by enhancing the general feeling of trust.

2.2 AI-generated information acceptance or avoidance

One of the biggest strengths of AI is the ability to acquire information and solve problems based on deep learning and knowledge creation and transfer (De Bruyn et al., 2020). With the capacity of AI to collect large amounts of data from individuals' history and interactions, AI recommendation engines can produce fast and relevant recommendations to reflect each person's preferences and wants. With AI's superb computational abilities, forecasts made by statistical models tend to outperform human instinct. For example, statistical models outperform human intuition in predicting student grades (Dawes, 1979), employee performance (Kuncel et al., 2013), and market demand (Sanders & Manrodt, 2003). With its large capacity of analyzing data and behavioral signals from customers, AI can transform those inputs into insights and improve personalized recommendations (Pardes, 2019). The increasing adoption of AI technology suggests that it provides greater performance and that consumers acknowledge and appreciate its unique capabilities.

Despite the superior performance and accuracy of judgments made by statistical models, the research of marketing and psychology documents people's skepticism toward accepting AI recommendations. As it is often the case with new and emerging technologies, individuals continue to have important reservations due to the significant levels of risk and uncertainty associated with those novelties (Seegebarth et al., 2019). Resistance, anxiety, and ambivalence toward new technologies represent important aspects of the success and adoption of those technologies (Talwar et al., 2020). Research showed that some people tend to prefer human recommendations over those made by statistical models (Castelo et al., 2018; Dietvorst et al., 2015; Longoni et al., 2019). The resistance of the statistical models' recommendations has been observed in various AI applications, including book suggestions (Yeomans et al., 2019), judgments for recruiters (Highhouse, 2008), predictions of employee performance (Kuncel et al., 2013) and crime (Kleinberg et al., 2017), consumer preferences (Sinha & Swearingen, 2001), medical decision-making (Longoni et al., 2019), and doctor preferences (Keeffe et al., 2005).

Prior research has investigated various task type factors that cause people to accept or avoid AI-generated information. One important aspect is the task accomplished by the AI systems. People avoid AI when the task is subjective and involves intuition or affect (Castelo, 2019). This is because AI is perceived to lack affective capabilities or empathy (Castelo et al., 2018). In addition, consumers avoid AI when the task domain involves intuitive (vs. analytical) aspects of decision-making (Jarrahi, 2018) or “common-sense” decisions (Guszcza et al., 2017; McAfee et al., 2012). This is related to the general belief that although AI shows superb performance and skills in analytical tasks (Castelo, 2019) and mechanical and analytical tasks, AI would be less functional in performing intuitive and empathetic tasks (Huang & Rust, 2018). The key characteristic of AI is its ability to process large amounts of data, identify data-driven patterns, and thus make appropriate and scientific recommendations (Marwala & Hurwitz, 2015). In the same vein, people tend not to associate AI applications with autonomous goals (Kim & Duhacheck, 2020) and will thus be less responsive to messages that highlight “why” rather than “how.” Indeed, people accept AI less for more consequential and risky tasks.

2.3 Preciseness and information processing

Marketers must understand the psychological factors that influence consumers' acceptance of AI-generated information and how to induce more favorable consumers' responses about their AI-generated information and marketing. As one novel way to increase AI technology acceptance among consumers, this study proposes that the number preciseness of the recommendation information will critically influence consumer responses to AI technology.

Various researchers have suggested that the preciseness of the information presented to someone can significantly influence their evaluation and judgment of that information. First, the impact of preciseness on information processing has been investigated most frequently in the pricing literature. One significant stream of research has involved comparing a price ending in “9” and a price ending in “0,” which relates to the preciseness of the information presented. Traditionally, researchers have found that consumers prefer prices with the precise ending (e.g., the “9” ending) to those with the less precise ending (e.g., the “0” ending). This phenomenon is mainly attributable to two explanations: (i) an association-based explanation (i.e., the tendency to associate a product with a price ending in “9” as the cheaper product) (Naipaul & Parsa, 2001; Stiving, 2000) and (ii) a heuristic process-based explanation (i.e., the tendency to ignore or drop-off the precise component; e.g., ignoring the “.99 cent” component of a $3.99 price value and instead considering it as $3) (Basu, 1997; Stiving & Winer, 1997). In addition, the preciseness of the information can influence the meaning of the price: Kim et al. (2020) suggested that people associate a precise price ($199) as a cost or sacrifice of the product, whereas they associate an imprecise price ($200) as a benefit or quality of the product.

Another stream of research (Burson et al., 2009; Gourville, 1998; Monga & Bagchi, 2012) has examined the different framing effects regarding the basic unit presented (e.g., 85 cents per day [pennies-a-day strategy] vs. $300 per year). Researchers have found that people prefer the product with precise (versus nonprecise) information, mainly due to the transaction utility (Gourville, 1998). When comparing two pieces of attribute information, people prefer the option with precise information (e.g., for a movie-rental plan—number of movies per week: seven and price per month: $10) to the same option with less precise information (e.g., number of movies per year: 364 and price per month: $10) (Burson et al., 2009). In short, the previous literature provides empirical evidence that the preciseness of the price and unit significantly influence our preference and value judgments.

Existing literature suggests two opposing conclusions regarding the impact of preciseness on the evaluation of the product. On the one hand, one stream of research shows the positive effect of precise information on product evaluation. According to this stream, the preciseness of information significantly influences confidence and credibility regarding that information (Jerez-Fernandez et al., 2014; Zhang & Schwarz, 2013). Indeed, people exhibit greater trust in other's evaluations when the estimation is precise (e.g., the estimated length of the Niger River in miles: 2611 miles) rather than less precise (e.g., 2600 miles) (Jerez-Fernandez et al., 2014). In addition, Xie and Kronrod (2012) found that people prefer product information using precise numbers (e.g., 19.42%) rather than that using less precise numbers (e.g., 20%). This effect was explained by the disfluency effect in that precise information requires elaborate processing, resulting in greater attention and evaluation. They further suggested that the positive impact of the precise information was only higher for people with low advertising skepticism. Put differently, people prefer precise information to aid their decision-making and judgment.

On the other hand, another research stream suggests a negative effect of precise information in evaluations. For example, a rounded (and less precise) number (e.g., 200 mg) is associated with the concept of stability as opposed to a precise number (e.g., 203 mg) (Pena-Marin & Bhargave, 2016). This difference also influences further inferred quality, such as the perception of the duration of the product benefits. In addition, less precise (vs. precise) numbers are easy to process and have higher processing fluency, resulting in more positive evaluations (King & Janiszewski, 2011; Wadhwa & Zhang, 2015). Furthermore, an imprecise number (e.g., $365,000) compared to a precise number (e.g., $364,578) could generate a feeling of uncertainty in the judgment and decision-making process. For example, Thomas et al. (2010) argued that a precise (vs. imprecise) number requires much deeper processing, which consequently evokes a feeling of uncertainty and therefore reduces product evaluations. In sum, the previous literature has suggested conflicting patterns regarding the impact of preciseness on evaluation.

2.4 Hypothesis 1: Precise format increases consumer response

By linking the literature about AI technology acceptance and the psychology of price preciseness, we make a novel prediction that the high (vs. low) preciseness of the AI-generated information derives positive consumer responses for the entities associated with the AI technology. We make this prediction based on the following. First, the existing literature suggests the belief of people that current AI has an excellent performance in automated and analytical tasks as opposed to intuitive and empathetic ones (Huang & Rust, 2018). We view that a salient characteristic of mechanical and analytical tasks is “precise” or “logical and analytic.” Similarly, people associate robots with agency-related capabilities (e.g., self-control, morality, memory, emotion recognition, planning, communication, and thought), which overlaps more closely with “preciseness” than experience-related capabilities (e.g., pleasure, personality, consciousness, pride, embarrassment, and joy) (Gray et al. (2007). Thus, we view that a precise format of AI-generated information would provide the notions of the performed task being analytic and automated, thereby resulting in more favorable consumer responses.

Second, given that consumers' confidence level is an essential factor for adopting new technology (Lee, 2004; Li et al., 2008), we argue that the preciseness format, which is associated with high confidence (Jerez-Fernandez et al., 2014; Zhang & Schwarz, 2013) will lead to favorable consumer responses. A competing hypothesis might suggest that information preciseness would elicit negative consumer responses because preciseness is associated with low processing fluency (King & Janiszewski, 2011; Wadhwa & Zhang, 2015). However, we view any negative influence due to low fluency to be minimal because given that AI is a new technology, consumers will process AI-generated information thoughtfully because. Therefore, the negative underlying mechanism of precise information (i.e., processing fluency) will be minimal for AI-involved decision-making and judgment, especially in a new environment. Based on this reasoning, we make the following hypothesis:

H1.Consumers show more favorable (a) behavioral intention and (b) evaluation toward the company when the AI-generated information is presented in a precise (vs. imprecise) format.

2.5 Hypothesis 2: Trust mediates the effect of precise format increasing consumer response

As the key mechanism for the information preciseness inducing favorable consumer response, consumers' perceived trust is the focus of this study. Several definitions of trust have emphasized the importance of the perceived confidence, honesty, and reliability of the other entity (Crosby et al., 1990; Morgan & Hunt, 1994). Indeed, Morgan and Hunt (1994) describe trust as the perception of “confidence in the exchange partner's reliability and integrity” (p. 23). Research also suggests that trust has both cognitive and affective dimensions. Cognitive trust relates more to the perception of the ability (e.g., skills, knowledge, and competencies), reliability, and integrity of the trustee, whereas the affective dimension involves the perceived benevolence and disposition to do good of the trustee (Johnson & Grayson, 2005; Mayer et al., 1995). Cognition related to available information and valid logic is the foundation of trust (Ye et al., 2020). Integrating these streams of research, given that crucial elements of trust are reliability and ability, we propose that the preciseness of AI-generated information will greatly strengthen trust toward the AI's recommendations, thus deriving in more favorable consumer responses toward the AI company.

Consumer trust is viewed as a significant factor for consumers' decision-making and behaviors, and researchers have put a lot of emphasis on understanding how to build and manage business strategies that contribute to a higher level of trust (Garbarino & Johnson, 1999; Morgan & Hunt, 1994). The importance of trust was investigated in several domains, such as communications, services marketing, branding, and sales management (Berry & Parasuraman, 1991; Eisingerich & Bell, 2008; Hovland et al., 1953; Sung & Kim, 2010).

Related to our research, past research has suggested that trust is a prominent factor in the development and adoption of new technologies and products. Building trust was a major factor for success in the development of relationships (Bart et al., 2005; Berry, 1995; Slade et al., 2015). Trust has also been a critical concept in academic research related to online and technology-mediated environments (Dimitriadis & Kyrezis, 2010; Schlosser et al., 2006; Yang et al., 2006). Emerging technologies are often associated with greater complexity and uncertainty, requiring confidence and trust in the systems (Seegebarth et al., 2019). Existing studies on recommendation agents have exposed the importance of trust in acceptance and reactance toward their recommendations (Aljukhadar et al., 2017; Wang & Benbasat, 2005).

We believe that the role of trust is especially strong in consumers' reactions toward AI companies or products. Trust is particularly critical when individuals lack control over the other party (Lynch et al., 2001). Indeed, compared to interpersonal relationships and traditional businesses, human/machine interactions require new means of creating trust, and more research is needed into how individuals place confidence in AI interactions (Bock et al., 2020). Compared to other technologies, AI has many unique and unfamiliar characteristics, and its performance and process need to be better defined. With the vulnerability related to AI, trust has been suggested to be a crucial antecedent of the development and successful adoption of AI (Ostrom et al., 2018).

Integrating the AI and trust literature, we suggest that the preciseness of AI-generated information will greatly strengthen trust toward the AI's recommendations, which will, in turn, lead to higher evaluations and intentions from individuals. More precise information is believed to be associated with a higher quality of communication and content. Therefore, this study proposes that trust will mediate the relationship between the preciseness of AI-generated information and consumer evaluations.

H2.The perceived trust for AI mediates the impact of the format preciseness of AI-generated information on consumer's (a) behavioral intention and (b) evaluation.

2.6 Present studies

Five studies tested our hypotheses in various contexts (see Figure 1 for the overall theoretical framework). Study 1 tested for initial evidence of the preciseness effect on consumers' response in the context of buying a company's stock. Study 2 tested for the mediating role of trust that explains the preciseness effect in an AI-based online music recommendation context. Studies 3 and 4 tested the boundary conditions of the preciseness effect; Study 3 tested whether the accuracy of AI-generated information moderates the impact of preciseness and Study 4 tested whether objective product quality influences the preciseness effect. Study 5 replicated the results of Study 1 with more defined measures (i.e., multiple measures) and using different experimental stimuli. See Table 1 for a detailed summary of our empirical studies.

3 STUDY 1

The purpose of Study 1 was to provide initial evidence of the preciseness effect of AI recommendations on consumers' responses (H1a). Participants evaluated AI-generated number information of a company's product, presented either in precise or imprecise format. We predicted that participants' responses toward the company would be more positive when the information was presented in a precise format.

3.1 Method

3.1.1 Pretest

To test the validity of our preciseness manipulation, a pretest was conducted (n = 51, Mage = 38.12, SD = 12.71; 49.1% female) with participants recruited via MTurk. Participants were randomly assigned to evaluate one of the two number information format (74.975% [precise] vs. 75% [imprecise]) that are used in the main study (see below section for details). Participants indicated their perceived preciseness of the accuracy rating of the AI system (i.e., “How detailed is the accuracy rating of the AI system?”) along two 7-point scales (1 = not at all precise/detailed, 7 = very precise/detailed; Pearson correlation r = 0.60, p < 0.001). Supporting the validity of using our experimental stimuli, the perceived preciseness was significantly higher for information format used in the precise condition (M = 4.84, SD = 1.09) than the imprecise condition (M = 4.41, SD = 1.40, F(1, 49) = 4.23, p = 0.045, η2 = 0.079).

3.1.2 Participants, design, and procedure

Participants were 114 (Mage = 37.38, SD = 12.99; 57.0% female) adults recruited via MTurk in exchange for a small monetary payment. The study employed a simple, one-way between-subjects experimental design with two levels (number presentation detail: precise vs. imprecise). All participants were asked to read the following passage containing information about a company's AI system that detects Coronavirus1:

A tech company has developed an AI (artificial intelligence) system that can detect Coronavirus on chest CT (computed tomography) scans. According to the researchers who developed the system, the AI system can yield a [74.975% vs. 75%] accuracy rate.

Participants were randomly assigned to either the precise condition in which they saw the 74.975% accuracy rate or the imprecise condition in which they say the 75% accuracy condition. Then, participants indicated their purchase intention to buy the company's stock (i.e., “If you have money to invest, to what extent are you willing to buy the stocks of the tech company?”) by answering on a 7-point scale (1 = not at all, 7 = very much). We viewed that purchase intention of company stock is an indicator of consumers' favorable evaluation and behavior toward the company and its products. Finally, participants provided their age and gender.

3.2 Results and discussion

We conducted an ANOVA and found a significant main effect of our experimental factor (F(1, 112) = 4.12, p = 0.045, η2 = 0.035). Specifically, participants in the precise condition (M = 4.28, SD = 1.71) showed higher intention to buy the company's stock than those in the imprecise condition (M = 3.63, SD = 1.71). These results support H1.

Study 1 provided initial evidence of the preciseness effect on consumers' responses toward the company associated with AI. As anticipated, participants showed more favorable responses toward the company when the AI-generated information was presented in a precise (vs. imprecise) format.

4 STUDY 2

The purpose of Study 2 was threefold. First, we wanted to replicate the preciseness effects in another marketing context: music. Participants indicated their attitudes and behavioral intentions toward AI-recommended songs, which were presented either in precise or imprecise format. We predicted more positive consumer responses when the AI information was given in a precise (vs. imprecise) format (H1a and H1b). Second, we measured consumer trust and tested whether it mediated the preciseness effect on consumer responses toward AI (H2). Third, we evaluated other consumer response measures: attitude toward the recommended songs and recommendation intention to others. One limitation of Study 1 was that the consumers' intention to purchase company stock was assessed as the key consumer response measure. Study 2 addresses this limitation by assessing multiple and more direct response measures.

4.1 Method

4.1.1 Pretest

A pretest was conducted (n = 82, Mage = 39.07, SD = 13.45; 49.1% female). After participants were randomly exposed to one of three experimental conditions (i.e., precise I [80.135%], precise II [80.000%], or the imprecise [80%]; see below section for details), they were asked to rate their perceived preciseness of the confidence rating of the AI system (i.e., “How detailed is the confidence rating of the AI recommendation system?”) along two 7-point scales (1 = not at all precise/detailed, 7 = very precise/detailed; Pearson correlation r = 0.59 p < 0.001). As expected, the perceived preciseness differed significantly between the experimental conditions (F(2, 79) = 6.82, p = 0.002, η2 = 0.147). Specifically, the perceived preciseness for the imprecise condition (M = 4.75, SD = 0.94) was lower than that for the precise I condition (M = 5.66, SD = 0.84; posthoc p = 0.002) and the precise II condition (M = 5.35, SD = 1.01; posthoc p = 0.046).

4.1.2 Participants, design, and procedure

Participants were 155 (Mage = 39.59, SD = 12.02; 47.1% female) adults recruited via MTurk. The study employed a one-way between-subjects experimental design with three levels (number presentation detail: precise I vs. precise II vs. imprecise). All participants were asked to read the following scenario about online music consumption:

Imagine that you visit an online music streaming site to listen and download some music in a specific genre. On the website, the AI (Artificial Intelligence)-based recommendation system shows you 3 songs that are "Recommended for You". The AI system came up with the list based on your past activities on the website. (The recommended songs are currently ranked #1, #3, #4, respectively, in the Hot 20 Chart list in the specific genre).

The AI recommendation system says it is [80.135% vs. 80.000% vs. 80%] confident that you will like these recommended songs.

The manipulation of the experimental factor was changed in that participants were randomly assigned to either the precise I (80.135%), precise II (80.000%), or the imprecise (80%) condition in the scenario above. The purpose of the precise II condition was to test the preciseness effect on evaluation not based on an absolute value difference but rather based on a difference in the specific presentation.

Then, participants indicated their attitudes toward the recommended songs (i.e., “To what extent do you think you will like the songs?”) and recommendation intentions to others (i.e., “To what extent are you willing to recommend the songs that AI recommended to your friend?”) by answering a 7-point scale (1 = not at all, 7 = very much). They were also asked to indicate their level of trust in the AI (i.e., “To what extent do you trust that AI knows your preference?”) by answering a 7-point scale (1 = not at all, 7 = very much). Finally, participants provided their age and gender.

4.2 Results and discussion

4.2.1 Main effects

First, we conducted a one-way ANOVA on the attitudes toward the recommended songs. The results indicated a marginally significant main effect of our experimental factor (F(2, 152) = 2.63, p = 0.075, η2 = 0.033). Planned contrast analysis found a significant difference between the imprecise (M = 4.67, SD = 1.58) and precise I (M = 5.19, SD = 0.98) conditions (p = 0.026). The difference between the imprecise and precise II conditions (M = 5.06, SD = 0.94) was not significant but did show the expected direction (p = 0.104). No difference emerged between the precise I and precise II conditions (p = 0.566) (Figure 2). These results support H1b.

Second, the results for the recommendation were similar in that the main effect was also significant (F(2, 152) = 3.54, p = 0.032, η2 = 0.044). Planned contrast analysis found significant differences between the imprecise (M = 4.02, SD = 1.52) and precise I (M = 4.72, SD = 1.37) conditions (p = 0.011) and between the imprecise and precise II conditions (M = 4.58, SD = 1.24; p = 0.049). No difference emerged between the precise I and precise II conditions (p = 0.576). These results replicated the results of Study 1 (H1a) in that consumers showed more favorable responses when the information was given in a precise (vs. imprecise) format.

Third, the results of AI trust perception indicated a marginally significant main effect of our experimental factor (F(2, 152) = 2.82, p = 0.063, η2 = 0.036). Planned contrast analysis found a significant difference between the imprecise (M = 4.33, SD = 1.60) and precise I (M = 4.91, SD = 1.08) conditions (p = 0.023), and a difference between the imprecise and precise II conditions (M = 4.79, SD = 1.14) that was marginally significant in the direction that is consistent with our hypothesis (p = 0.080). No difference emerged between the precise I and precise II conditions (p = 0.606).

4.2.2 Mediation analysis

For mediation analysis, we collapsed the two conditions into the precise condition and compared them to the imprecise condition because precise I and II conditions do not show differences. The results indicated significant differences for attitude (M_imprecise = 4.67, SD = 1.58, M_precise = 5.13, SD = 0.96; F(1, 153) = 4.93, p = 0.028, η2 = 0.031), recommendation intentions (M_imprecise = 4.02, SD = 1.52, M_precise = 4.65, SD = 1.31; F(1, 153) = 6.79, p = 0.010, η2 = 0.042), and AI trust (M_imprecise = 4.33, SD = 1.60, M_precise = 4.85, SD = 1.11; F(1, 153) = 5.40, p = 0.021, η2 = 0.034).

Using Hayes' (2017) Process Macro (Model 4 with 5000 bootstrapping), we conducted a simple mediation (i.e., IV: preciseness of→Mediator: AI trust→DV: recommendation intention). The results indicated that the indirect effect was significant (effect = 0.33, 95% confidence interval [CI]: [0.008, 0.681]), whereas the direct effect was not significant (effect = 0.30, t = 1.51, 95% CI: [−0.094, 0.701]), indicating a full mediation (H2a). The additional simple mediation (i.e., IV: preciseness of→Mediator: AI trust→DV: attitude) was also significant (effect = 0.32, 95% CI: [0.021, 0.661]), whereas the direct effect was not significant (effect = 0.14, t = 0.51, 90% CI: [−0.172, 0.457]), indicating a full mediation (H2b).

Based on the results above, we conducted a serial mediation (i.e., IV: preciseness of information [1: imprecise, 2: precise]→Mediator 1: AI trust→Mediator 2: attitude→DV: recommendation intention) using Hayes' (2017) Process Macro (Model 6 with 5000 bootstrapping). The results indicated that the direct effect with mediators became insignificant (effect = 0.25, t = 1.28, 95% CI: [−0.133, 0.624]). By contrast, the overall mediation was significant (effect = 0.39, 95% CI: [0.031, 0.789]). Specifically, the serial mediation (i.e., preciseness of information→AI trust→attitude→recommendation intention) was significant (effect = 0.13, 95% CI: [0.005, 0.326]), as was one of the simple mediation (i.e., preciseness of information→AI trust→recommendation intention) (effect = 0.20, 95% CI: [0.011, 0.453]). However, another simple mediation (i.e., the preciseness of information→attitude→recommendation intention) was not significant (effect = 0.06, 95% CI: [−0.066, 0.239]), suggesting the role of AI trust as the key mediator.

In Study 2, we provided another evidence of the preciseness effect of AI recommendations in another context (i.e., online music services). As expected, participants' attitude toward the products associated with AI and behavioral intention (i.e., recommendation to others) were more favorable when the information was given in a precise rather than an imprecise format.

Second, we further provided empirical evidence of the critical role that consumer trust toward AI mediates the preciseness effect on consumer response. Third, we showed the preciseness effect influenced consumers' evaluation (i.e., attitude) and behavioral responses (i.e., recommendation to others) about the AI company's products.

5 STUDY 3

The purpose of Study 3 was to test for boundary conditions of the preciseness effect to examine more circumstances when preciseness will lead to more adoption through trust. Research on online recommendations suggests that accuracy influences the adoption of new technologies (Punj & Moore, 2007; Zhu et al., 2014). In relation to AI, data accuracy is also a critical factor determining its adoption by individuals. The accuracy and quality of the information available to people influence consumers' responses (Kreps & Neuhauser, 2013). Therefore, we predict that the preciseness effect of the information is more pronounced when the accuracy of the information is lower. Indeed, we suggest that when the difference between estimations of AI and individuals are bigger (vs. smaller), the preciseness of the information will have a stronger influence on decisions to adopt the AI estimations.

H3.When the accuracy of the information is low (vs. high), information preciseness has a stronger influence on consumers' responses.

5.1 Method

5.1.1 Pretest

Aa separate pretest was conducted (n = 53, Mage = 38.55, SD = 14.82; 49.1% female). After participants were randomly exposed to one of the eight experimental conditions (see below section for details), they were asked to rate their perceived preciseness of the accuracy of the AI system (i.e., How detailed is the estimation of the AI system?”) along two 7-point scales (1 = not at all precise/detailed, 7 = very precise/detailed; Pearson correlation r = 0.84, p < 0.001). As expected, the overall effect was significant (F(7, 45) = 2.43, p = 0.034, η2 = 0.274). The further analysis, after considering two groups (1: imprecise conditions, 2: precise condition), also indicated that the perceived preciseness was significantly higher for the precise condition (M = 5.43, SD = 1.41) than it was for the imprecise condition (M = 4.02, SD = 1.77, F(1, 51) = 10.28, p = 0.002, η2 = 0.168).

5.1.2 Participants, design, and procedure

Participants were 436 (Mage = 38.39, SD = 12.96; 46.8% female) adults recruited via MTurk in exchange for a small monetary payment. The study employed a 2 (number presentation detail: precise vs. imprecise) × 2 (estimation direction: low vs. high to the actual weight) × 2 (estimation accuracy: similar to the actual weight vs. different from the actual weight) between-subjects design.

The stimuli were modified from the stimuli used by Logg et al. (2019). Specifically, participants were asked to estimate the weight of a person in a provided photo ​from Logg et al. (2019) (see Appendix A for the photo). Participants were first asked to read the following:

An AI (artificial intelligence) system incorporating machine learning estimated the weight of the person in the photograph to be [130 vs. 130.25 vs. 160 vs. 160.25 vs. 169.75 vs. 170 vs. 189.75 vs. 190] lb.

The manipulation of the experimental factor was changed in that participants were randomly assigned to either the precise conditions (i.e., 130, 160, 170, and 190 lb) or the imprecise conditions (i.e., 130.25, 160.25, 169.75, and 189.75 lb) group in the scenario above. The estimation direction was lower (i.e., 130, 130.25, 160, and 160.25 lb) than the actual weight or estimated weight (i.e., actual weight = 164 lb and estimated weight from separate pretest [n = 85] = 162.96 lb, SD = 16.83), or higher (i.e., 169.75, 170, 189.75, and 190 lb) than both these weights. The estimation accuracy was manipulated by providing numbers either similar to the actual weight (i.e., 160, 160.25, 169.75, and 169.75 lb) or different from the actual weight (i.e., 130, 130.25, 189.75, and 190 lb).

Participants were then asked to estimate the weight considering the information above. Therefore, the key dependent variable was the weight estimation. We assumed that if the participants believed the AI recommendation, they would generate a biased estimation from the actual weight or estimated weight without any information (i.e., 164–165 lb). Finally, participants were asked to provide their age and gender.

5.2 Results and discussion

Based on H3, we expect the below predictions from our experiment. Specifically, the impact of estimation accuracy will moderate the impact of estimated direction on the relationship between information preciseness and consumers' response (i.e., the estimated weight of a person). On the one hand, when the estimated weight is significantly different from the actual weight, the significant interaction effect between estimation direction and preciseness of AI information will be significant in that participants' estimated weight will be lower when AI estimated weight is precise (vs. imprecise) when AI estimation is lower than the actual one. Meanwhile, participants' estimated weight will be higher when AI estimated weight is precise (vs. imprecise) when AI estimation is higher than the actual one. On the other hand, when the estimated weight is similar to the actual weight, the interaction effect between estimation direction and preciseness of AI information on the participants' estimated weight will not be significant.

To verify these predictions, we conducted a 2 (number presentation detail) × 2 (estimation direction) × 2 (estimation accuracy) ANOVA on the estimated weight. The results indicated that the three-way interaction effect was significant (F(1, 427) = 5.78, p = 0.017, η2 = 0.013). To illustrate the three-way interaction effect, we conducted two separate 2 (number presentation detail) × 2 (estimation direction) ANOVA for weights similar to the actual weight condition and weights different from the actual weight condition.

First, for the different from the actual weight condition (i.e., 130, 130.25, 189.75, and 190 lb), a 2 (number presentation detail) × 2 (estimation direction) ANOVA generated a significant two-way interaction effect (F(1, 208) = 8.58, p = 0.004, η2 = 0.040). Planned contrast analysis found that when the AI's estimation was lower (i.e., 130 and 130.25 lb) than the actual weight condition, the estimated weight was lower (showed higher biased estimation) for the precise condition (M = 143.61 lb, SD = 16.92) than the imprecise condition (M = 150.78 lb, SD = 18.19, F(1, 208) = 5.67, p = 0.018, η2 = 0.027). By contrast, the opposite pattern was found for the conditions when the AI estimation was higher (i.e., 189.75 and 190 lb) than the actual weight condition. The participants' estimated weights were higher (showed higher biased estimation) for the precise condition (M = 178.56 lb, SD = 12.32) than the imprecise condition (M = 172.94 lb, SD = 15.19, F(1, 208) = 3.16, p = 0.077, η2 = 0.015) (Figure 3). In sum, we found that precise AI recommendations generated estimations that were more biased by the participants. This tendency indicated the participants' beliefs regarding AI-generated information.

Second, for the similar to the actual weight condition (i.e., 160, 160.25, 169.75, and 169.75 lb), the overall two-way interaction effect was not significant (F(1, 208) = 0.02, p = 0.881, η2 = 0.001). The detailed pattern is presented in Figure 3. In sum, we found a significant three-way interaction effect showing important moderators for our main hypotheses. When the AI-generated information was similar to the layperson's estimation, the preciseness of the AI-generated information did not matter. However, when the AI-generated information was substantially different from the layperson's estimation, the preciseness of the AI-generated information did matter. In this situation, the preciseness of the information helped individuals adopt the AI's recommendation, even in cases of biased estimation, which supports our prediction about the importance of information preciseness when the accuracy of the information is lower. These results support H3.

6 STUDY 4

The purpose of Study 4 was to test another boundary condition to examine when the preciseness of information is more critical. In this experiment, we investigate whether the number presentation detail associated with AI and the objective product quality of the recommended products influenced consumer purchase intentions. In addition, we tested whether consumer trust toward AI mediated the effects of presentation detail and objective quality on purchase intention for a different product category (i.e., books). Previous research demonstrates that the objective quality of a decision or a product has a significant impact on information processing in the case of recommendation agents (Gupta & Harris, 2010). In addition, price preciseness has been demonstrated to influence the perceived quality of product and ultimately affecting consumers' preferences (Kim & Kim, & Marshall, 2020).

H4.When the product's objective quality is high (vs. how), information preciseness has a stronger influence on consumers' purchase intentions.

6.1 Method: Participants, design, and procedure

Participants were 157 (Mage = 36.80, SD = 11.60; 46.5% female) adults recruited via MTurk. The study employed a 2 (number presentation detail: precise vs. imprecise) × 2 (objective product quality: high vs. low) between-subjects design. Participants were randomly assigned to either the precise (79.865%) or the imprecise (80%) condition and either the high objective quality (ranking #1, #2, and #4) or the low objective quality (ranking #11, #12, and #14) condition. All participants read the following scenario about purchasing books online:

Imagine that you visit an online bookstore to look for some books in a specific category. As you are starting to browse, the AI (Artificial Intelligence)-based recommendation system shows you 3 books that “Recommended for You.” The AI system came up with the list based on your past activities at the website.

The recommended books, which you have not read, are currently ranked [#1, #2, #4 vs. #11, #12, #14], respectively, in the Top 20 Bestsellers list in the specific category.

The AI recommendation system says it is [79.865% vs. 80%] confident that you will like these recommended books.

Then, participants indicated their purchase intention by answering two questions, “To what extent are you willing to consider buying the books that AI recommended?” and “To what extent are you willing to buy the books that AI recommended?” (1 = not at all, 7 = very much). The responses to these two purchase intention items were averaged (Pearson correlation r = 0.78, p < 0.001).

Participants indicated their levels of AI trust by answering two questions, “To what extent do you trust that AI knows your preference?” and “To what extent do you trust that AI correctly assessed your preference?” (1 = not at all, 7 = very much; Pearson correlation r = 0.83, p < 0.001).

Participants answered the question, “How detailed is the confidence rating of the AI recommendation system?” (1 = not at all detailed, 7 = very detailed), as a manipulation check for the number presentation manipulation. Meanwhile, for the objective quality manipulation, participants answered the question, “According to the scenario, how high are the recommended books ranked in the top 20 Bestseller list?” (1 = not at all high, 7 = very high). Finally, participants were asked to provide their age and gender.

6.2 Results

6.2.1 Manipulation checks

A series of 2 × 2 ANOVA analysis revealed that experimental manipulations were successful. As predicted, a 2 × 2 ANOVA on perceived detail revealed only a marginal main effect of the number presentation (Mprecise = 5.33, SD = 1.20; Mimprecise = 4.96, SD = 1.49, F(1, 153) = 2.90, p = 0.091, η2 = 0.019), whereas a 2 × 2 ANOVA on perceived product quality revealed only a main effect of the product quality (Mhigh = 6.15, SD = 1.06; Mlow = 4.24, SD = 1.88, F(1, 153) = 61.10, p < 0.001, η2 = 0.285). Thus, our manipulations were successful.

6.2.2 Results and discussion

Next, a 2 × 2 ANOVA on purchase intention revealed only a significant interaction effect (F(1, 153) = 4.24, p = 0.041, η2 = 0.027) (Figure 4). Planned contrast analysis revealed that in the high objective quality condition, the precise presentation led to higher purchase intention (M = 5.18, SD = 1.28) than the imprecise presentation (M = 4.46, SD = 1.73; F(1, 153) = 5.32, p = 0.022, η2 = 0.034). However, in the low objective quality condition, the precise presentation (M = 4.70, SD = 1.17) and the imprecise presentation (M = 4.88, SD = 1.32; F (1, 153) = 0.36, p = 0.550, η2 = 0.002) did not differ in terms of purchase intention. These results support H4.

A 2 × 2 ANOVA on AI trust revealed only a significant interaction effect (F(1, 153) = 5.46, p = 0.021, η2 = 0.034). Planned contrast analysis revealed that in the high objective quality condition, the precise presentation led to higher AI trust (M = 4.88, SD = 1.20) than the imprecise presentation (M = 4.16, SD = 1.66; F(1, 153) = 5.18, p = 0.024, η2 = 0.033). However, in the low objective quality condition, the precise presentation (M = 4.55, SD = 1.19) and the imprecise presentation (M = 4.87, SD = 1.47; F(1, 153) = 1.05, p = 0.308, η2 = 0.007) did not differ in terms of AI trust.

Using Hayes' (2017) Process Macro (Model 8 with 5000 bootstrapping), a moderated mediation analysis was conducted (i.e., IV: preciseness of information [1: imprecise, 2: precise]→Mediator: AI trust→DV: purchase intention & Moderator: objective product quality [1: high, 2: low]). The results indicated that AI trust mediated the effect of Number Presentation × Objective Product Quality on purchase intention (95% CI: [−1.587, −0.126]). Specifically, for the high-quality recommendation condition, trust significantly mediated the impact of IV on DV (95% CI: [0.055, 1.138]), consistent with H2a. By contrast, the corresponding mediation was not significant for the low-quality recommendation condition (95% CI: [−0.743, 0.224]).

Study 4 tested the effects of number presentation detail on AI confidence and of objective product quality of the recommended products on consumers' purchase intention. As predicted, we found that the precise presentation led to higher purchase intention than the imprecise presentation when the objective quality of the recommended products was high. However, as predicted, the number presentation did not influence purchase intention when the objective quality of the recommended products was relatively low. In addition, we showed that consumer trust toward AI mediated the effect of presentation details on purchase intention.

7 STUDY 5

The purpose of Study 5 was to address some limitations from previous studies. First, even though we provided the empirical evidence of mediation in the prior studies (i.e., Studies 2 and 4), the key mediator of trust was measured by a single item. In this study, we will use the multiple-item scale based on Kim et al. (2016).

Second, in all previous studies, the imprecise numbers were based on a multiple of 5 or 10 (e.g., 75%, 80%, 130 lb, or 190 lb). This study will use a precise number using multiple of 5 and an imprecise number without using multiple of 5. Specifically, we will compare an imprecise number (i.e., 78%) and a precise number (i.e., 78.025%). Finally, we will reduce the weaknesses of Study 1 by measuring the overall attitude toward the product and the intention to buy the company's stock.

7.1 Method: Participants, design, and procedure

Participants were 131 (Mage = 41.40, SD = 12.75; 51.9% female) adults recruited via MTurk in exchange for a small monetary payment. The study employed a one-way between-subjects experimental design with two levels (number presentation detail: precise vs. imprecise). The overall stimuli were similar to that of Study 1A with few modifications. First, the accuracy of the CT scan system was either 78% [imprecise condition] or 78.025% [precise condition].

Then, participants were asked to indicate their perceived trust of AI-based CT scan system by three-item 7-point scale (1 = not at all TOKEN, 7 = very TOKEN, Cronbach's α = 0.923, based on Kim et al. (2016)). They also indicated the overall attitude toward the CT scan system by 3-item 7-point scale (1 = not at all good/favorable/positive, 7 = very good/favorable/positive, Cronbach's α = 0.949, based on Kim et al. (2016)) and their intention to buy the company's stock with the same scale of Study 1. Participants were asked to rate their perceived preciseness of the accuracy rating of the AI system (i.e., How detailed is the accuracy rating of the AI system?”) along a 7-point scale (1 = not at all detailed, 7 = very detailed) for the manipulation check. Finally, participants were asked to provide their age and gender.

7.2 Results and discussion

The manipulation was successful in that the perceived preciseness was higher for the precise condition (M = 5.09, SD = 1.02) compared to the imprecise condition (M = 4.51, SD = 1.32; F(1, 129) = 8.00, p = 0.005, η2 = 0.058).

We compared consumers' responses across the two experimental conditions (see Figure 5). First, the intention to buy the company's stock in that the intention for the precise condition (M = 4.68, SD = 1.82) was higher than that for imprecise condition (M = 3.91, SD = 1.83; F(1, 129) = 5.92, p = 0.016, η2 = 0.044). These results support H1a. Next, supporting H1b, the result of the attitude toward the scan system was similar in that the attitude for the precise condition (M = 5.31, SD = 1.21) was higher than that for the imprecise condition (M = 4.65, SD = 1.41; F(1, 129) = 8.47, p = 0.004, η2 = 0.062).

For the mediator, we found that the perceived trust of the AI-based scan system for the precise condition (M = 5.17, SD = 1.16) was higher than that for the imprecise condition (M = 4.75, SD = 1.25; F(1, 129) = 3.85, p = 0.052, η2 = 0.029).

Finally, we conducted two mediation tests. We conducted a simple mediation (i.e., IV: preciseness of→Mediator: AI trust→DV: intention to buy the company's stock) using Hayes' (2017) Process Macro (Model 4 with 5000 bootstrapping). The results indicated that the indirect effect was significant (effect = 0.33, 95% CI: [0.013, 0.729]), whereas the direct effect was not significant (effect = 0.45, t = 1.62, 95% CI: [−0.099, 0.996]), indicating a full mediation (H2a). Next, another simple mediation (i.e., IV: preciseness of information [1: imprecise, 2: precise]→Mediator: perceived trust→DV: attitude) using Hayes' (2017) Process Macro (Model 4 with 5000 bootstrapping). The results showed that mediation was significant (effect = 0.37, 95% CI: [0.014, 0.772]), whereas the direct effect was also still significant (effect = 0.30, t = 2.24, 95% CI: [0.035, 0.556]). These results support H2b.

The second analysis was a serial mediation (i.e., IV: preciseness of information [1: imprecise, 2: precise]→Mediator 1: perceived trust→Mediator 2: attitude→DV: intention to buy the company's stock) using Hayes' (2017) Process Macro (Model 6 with 5000 bootstrapping). The results indicated that the direct effect with mediators became insignificant (effect = 0.22, t = 0.83, 95% CI: [−0.302, 0.739]), but the serial mediation was significant (effect = 0.29, 95% CI: [0.003, 0.621]).

8 GENERAL DISCUSSION

AI uses various key technologies, including machine learning and deep learning, and it can efficiently process vast amounts of data and learn relationships among the data. AI is becoming an important tool in marketing and business applications. In fact, AI is revolutionizing how companies and organizations operate businesses. With the increasing presence of AI in several aspects of the business environment and our daily lives, organizations and marketers are keen to find ways to improve the successful adoption of AI technologies and AI-generated recommendations. Despite the superior performance and accuracy of AI, recent evidence has shown that consumers have reservations toward accepting AI-generated recommendations (Castelo et al., 2018; Dietvorst et al., 2015; Longoni et al., 2019). Therefore, it is critical to understand how to improve consumer adoption of the information generated by AI.

The current research uses five studies to provide insights into how one nudging way (i.e., the preciseness of the AI-generated information) of presenting the information can influence consumer evaluations and intentions. Taken together, the results of the five experiments contribute to the understanding of AI-generated information and the continuous development of trust in AI technologies. First, we demonstrate a relationship between the preciseness of the information and individuals' evaluations of AI recommendations. Indeed, precise (vs. imprecise) information leads to higher evaluations and behavioral intentions (Studies 1–5). This effect is consistent across different measurements, dependent variables, and recommendation categories (i.e., coronavirus chest CT scans, AI-based online music recommendation engine, AI-based weight estimator, AI-based online book recommendation engine). This study also provides evidence that the impact of information preciseness on consumer evaluations is mediated by trust (Studies 2, 4, and 5). Finally, this study demonstrates the boundary conditions to this relationship. The accuracy of the AI-generated information influences the impact of the presented information (Study 3). Further, the objective product quality of the recommended products impacts the effect of presentation details (i.e., preciseness) on consumer purchase intentions (Study 4).

8.1 Theoretical contributions

These findings make several important theoretical contributions to the AI literature. First, this study offers new insights into the AI literature by investigating a new factor that influences consumer attitudes and behaviors regarding the adoption of AI recommendations. Our findings are consistent with prior work on the preciseness of information and price, suggesting that preciseness significantly influences individuals' preferences and value judgments (Gourville, 1998). It may be argued that being exposed to precise information may further affect one's confidence in the information. Our study investigates this concept in the AI environment to build on the aforementioned work by demonstrating that precise information (vs. imprecise information) can lead to more positive evaluations. Regarding the positive effect of precise numbers in terms of quantitative evaluation and judgment, Zhang and Schwarz (2013) suggested that the influence of precise information is greater in that the relevant inference (e.g., the estimated cost of a DVD drive) was higher when the related information was delivered in the precise versus imprecise format (e.g., when the retailing price of the DVD drive was $29.75 vs. $40). They further argue that this positive effect is only valid for human communicators and not for non-human agents like computer messages. Contrary to this result, we find that the preciseness of information generated by non-human agents (i.e., AI) significantly influences the evaluation of and inference related to a recommendation. One possible explanation for this is that people might consider AI to be closer to human-like agents than simple computers (see Gray et al., 2007). Future research can investigate this possible moderating factor for the preciseness effect.

Second, prior work suggests that trust is a crucial factor in the adoption of new technologies, thus advancing trust as a critical element for understanding people's behaviors toward AI. Specifically, we add to this study by showing that precise information increases the level of confidence that individuals place in the relevant information and that this trust leads to positive affective and behavioral outcomes. This finding offers empirical support for arguments suggesting that increased preciseness of information leads to higher reliability and credibility. Our research shows that in contrast to imprecise information, precise information may elicit people to think that the information is more reliable, potentially elevating their trust level.

Finally, it extends the literature on heuristics by showing that AI-generated recommendations can be used as an anchor in consumer judgments and decision-making processes. In the contexts of the TOKEN heuristic (Tversky & Kahneman, 1974), Janiszewski and Uy (2008) found that the adjustment from the given anchor was weaker when the anchoring information was based on precise (vs. less precise) information. For example, the price estimation of a type of cheese was lower when the anchor, which was higher than the actual price, was less precise (e.g., “5”) rather than precise (e.g., “4.85”). This study extends this pattern into the context of judgments of AI-generated information. Therefore, even in the context of nonhuman information, the AI recommendation served as anchoring information in the judgment. We also suggested the boundary conditions, such as the gap between the AI-generated information and the actual information, in Study 3.

8.2 Managerial implications

This paper has several practical and managerial implications. First, the results of our experiments provide additional evidence that the presentation of the information provided by AI recommendation agents can strongly impact consumer adoption of AI-related technologies. The design and delivery of the generated information are of critical importance for businesses using AI. As AI is becoming increasingly prevalent, companies can influence its adoption in our everyday lives. AI recommendations can be useful for consumers by making their lives easier and providing an enhanced experience. Thus, understanding more about the factors that impact individuals' interactions with AI can lead to faster and stronger acceptance of this new technology.

Second, according to the findings of this study, trust is an essential factor in consumer evaluations of AI-generated information. Indeed, trust mediates the relationship between the preciseness of information and judgments and evaluations of that information. This should be considered by marketers and information system leaders developing and managing AI technologies. Based on the findings of this study, we recommend that businesses should consider factors, such as preciseness, in the presentation of information, which could lead to higher credibility. Such nudges should increase the level of trust and thus motivate people to adopt the recommendations.

Finally, the recommendations generated by the AI agents and presented to consumers are crucial elements that affect their judgments; therefore, businesses should have valid and accurate models to develop the information they deliver in these recommendations. Thus, the algorithms developed with the data to find patterns must be correct to form strong relationships with consumers, as the validity and reliability of those suggestions will determine the future adoption of AI-generated recommendations.

8.3 Limitations and future directions

This study has several limitations, which can offer opportunities for future work. First, this study was administered through online panels providing experimental scenarios. Although hypothetical scenarios have been used in the past in the AI area, further research needs to be done to replicate these results in real interactions with AI recommendation agents and technologies. Regarding the Amazon Mturk sample, previous research suggested that the overall quality of Mturk was good as other sources of marketing research (e.g., Buhrmester et al., 2011; Crump et al., 2013), even though their understanding of AI and machine learning could be different from other populations. Future studies can cover this issue much deeply.

Second, as presentation methods are diverse, we can assume that their impacts on persuading individuals will differ (e.g., Giroux et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2020, 2021). Therefore, future research should employ additional presentation methods and determine their influences on customers' reactions.

Third, even though our paper focused on the AI recommendation, it's worthy to investigate the impact of the preciseness of human suggestions. We expect that the impact of the preciseness would be weaker for humans, but it might depend on the expertise area of a human (e.g., Huang & Rust, 2018). Finally, specific characteristics or factors (e.g., persuasion knowledge model; Friestad & Wright, 1994; Kim et al., 2016) could influence trust; hence, different antecedents of trust related to AI-generated content and recommendations represent a promising field for future research.

8.4 Closing statements

In this paper, we investigated a factor that can facilitate the adoption of AI-powered technology. Specifically, we suggested that the employment of precise (vs. imprecise) information in AI-generated messages leads to higher evaluations and behavioral intentions for that AI technology. We hope that this study helps extend the understanding of the psychological reasons affecting consumer adoption of AI technology.
